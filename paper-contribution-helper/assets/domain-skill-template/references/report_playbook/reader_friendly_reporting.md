# Reader-Friendly Reporting Contract

The report must be easier to read without reducing content. Every abstract packaging idea should be written in this order:

1. Plain-language version: what this means in ordinary words.
2. Technical thesis: the paper-ready research claim.
3. Concrete example or analogy: a small example, mechanism analogy, or "if a reviewer asks X, this route answers Y".
4. Evidence anchor: table, figure, appendix item, method section, or explicit claim from the target PDF.
5. Boundary: what the author must not overclaim.

Use short paragraphs before tables. Tables are for comparison and auditability, not for replacing explanation.

For story routes, never output only route names such as "broken assumption route" or "evidence-system route". Explain:

- what problem the route solves for the author;
- why reviewers may accept this route more readily than the current framing;
- what the author would actually rewrite;
- one concrete example of how the route sounds in paper language;
- when the route is unsafe.

The default reader is the paper author, not another reviewer. Use direct, practical wording.
